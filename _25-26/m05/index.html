About the Game

Dark Souls is an action role-playing game developed by FromSoftware and published by Bandai Namco in 2011. Set in the crumbling kingdom of Lordran, the game plunges players into a dying world on the edge of an age of darkness. Unlike most games of its era, Dark Souls tells its story almost entirely through item descriptions, environmental details, and cryptic NPC dialogue — rewarding players who pay close attention and punishing those who don't.

The world is interconnected in a way that was revolutionary at the time. Areas loop back on themselves, shortcuts unlock gradually, and the geography rewards exploration far more than any waypoint marker ever could. If you're new to the series and want to learn more before diving in, check out the community at r/darksouls or the comprehensive Fextralife Wiki.

Why I Love It

Dark Souls taught me patience. Every death is a lesson, not a punishment. The game never explains its mechanics beyond the barest minimum, which means every discovery — a shortcut, a hidden weapon, a boss weakness — feels genuinely earned. There is no handholding, no difficulty slider, and no apology for being hard.

The design philosophy behind Dark Souls influenced an entire generation of games. Titles like Hollow Knight, Sekiro, Elden Ring, and dozens of others owe a significant debt to the framework FromSoftware built here. Playing it now, years after release, still holds up — both mechanically and atmospherically. The sense of loneliness and quiet dread it creates is unmatched. You can read more about its legacy on Wikipedia.

More than anything, it respects the player's intelligence. It assumes you can figure things out. That alone makes it stand apart from nearly every other game I've played.

6 Things You Should Know About Dark Souls

  • The game was directed by Hidetaka Miyazaki, who nearly quit game development before FromSoftware hired him.
  • Death is a core mechanic — you drop your souls (currency/XP) on death and must retrieve them before dying again.
  • The entire world is one connected map with no loading screens between most areas.
  • Nearly every NPC has a questline that can fail silently if you do things in the wrong order.
  • The lore is never told directly — it is pieced together through item descriptions, boss names, and world design.
  • The game's infamous difficulty is intentional: Miyazaki wanted players to feel the triumph of overcoming adversity.
